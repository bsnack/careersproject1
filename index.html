Your High School Survival Guide

From a Grade 10 student who's been there! 📚✨

Hey there, future high schooler!

Remember when you were scared of riding a bike without training wheels? High school is kind of like that. It seems scary at first, but when you get the hang of it, it's actually pretty fun!

I'm Sebastian, and I just finished Grade 10. I remember being really nervous about going into a new setting, with nobody I knew, but now I'm here to share the 5 most important things that made my transition smooth, and even fun!

1

Map Out Your School

The most stressing factor in Grade 9 was getting lost. High school is much larger than elementary school or middle school, and you'll have different classrooms for each individual subject, so you should become familiar with your school by the end of your first week.

A Small Story

On my first day, I spent 15 minutes looking for my math class and ended up just walking in circles around the library because the school was so large. That's when I decided to actually look at the floor plan and become familiar with the place.

What You Should Do:
  • Visit your school during orientation day and walk the halls
  • Find the quickest routes AND backup routes
  • Locate all the bathrooms (trust me, you'll need this info!)
2

Learn How to Efficiently Manage Time

In elementary school, your teacher helped you stay organized. In high school, YOU'RE the one in charge of your schedule (not in school but at home)! This was probably the biggest change for me. You'll have homework from multiple teachers, extracurriculars, and maybe even a part-time job.

When I Thought I Could Just Wing It

In my first few months, I thought I could remember everything in my head. I was incredibly wrong. I forgot about a major project in one of my classes, and had to almost pull an all-nighter. From then on, I started to use the reminders app, which helps me stay on track.

What You Should Do:
  • Get a planner or use your phone's calendar app
  • Write down ALL due dates as soon as you get them
  • Break big projects into smaller, manageable tasks
  • Set reminders 2-3 days before deadlines
  • Schedule study time like you would a dentist appointment
  • Leave buffer time for unexpected stuff (it WILL happen!)
3

Build a Friend Group

High school can feel overwhelming at times, but you don't need to do it alone. Building a support system consisting of friends, teachers and mentors was incredibly helpful for me. These people, if chosen right, will celebrate your wins and help you through tougher times.

How My English Teacher Saved My Grade

I was struggling with essay writing in Grade 9. Instead of suffering in silence, I asked my English teacher for help. She gave me extra feedback and even recommended some writing resources. My grades improved, and I gained confidence!

What You Should Do:
  • Introduce yourself to teachers - they WANT to help you succeed
  • Join at least one club or sport to meet like-minded people
  • Don't be afraid to ask questions (everyone else probably has the same ones!)
  • Find a study partner in each of your classes
  • Connect with older students who can share their experiences
  • Remember: asking for help is a sign of strength, not weakness
4

Set Goals That Actually Matter to YOU

Everyone will have opinions about what you should do in high school, whether it be to enjoy it, to get high grades, or to work hard to get into a dream university. But the thing is, your goals should reflect what YOU care about, not what everybody else does or expects. Start with small achievable goals and go from there.

Setting Goals

At first, I set a goal to get at least a 95% in every subject because that's what my friends were doing and I thought it would be easily achievable. But I was wrong. It wasn't middle school or elementary school anymore. I realized I cared more about perfect grades than understanding concepts. I changed my goal to "truly understand each subject" and ended up doing better AND feeling happier.

What You Should Do:
  • Set 2-3 realistic goals for your first semester
  • Make them specific and measurable (not just "do well")
  • Include both academic and personal goals
  • Write them down and review them monthly
  • Celebrate small wins along the way
  • Be flexible - it's okay to adjust goals as you grow
5

Take Care of Yourself First

This might be the most important tip of all! High school brings new pressures, and it's easy to get caught up in trying to be perfect. But remember - you're still growing and learning. Be kind to yourself, and don't forget to have fun!

Avoid Burnout

In my second semester, I was doing homework until midnight every night and skipping lunch to study. I got sick, my grades dropped, and I was cranky all the time. That's when I learned that taking care of myself wasn't selfish - it was necessary!

What You Should Do:
  • Get 7-9 hours of sleep (your brain needs it to learn!)
  • Eat regular meals and stay hydrated
  • Make time for activities you enjoy
  • Talk to someone when you're feeling stressed
  • Remember that mistakes are part of learning
  • Don't compare yourself to others - you're on your own journey
